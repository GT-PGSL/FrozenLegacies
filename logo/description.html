Project Description

Exploring Four Decades of Change Beneath Antarctica’s Ross Ice Shelf

See Executive Summary

Executive Summary

Frozen Legacies represents an unprecedented investigation of Antarctica's Ross Ice Shelf, combining newly digitized 1970s radar film with modern airborne surveys to provide the first direct measurements of ice shelf changes over nearly half a century.

This collaborative initiative between Georgia Tech, Stanford, and Colorado School of Mines addresses critical knowledge gaps in ice shelf evolution. By processing, calibrating, and analyzing over 400,000 line-kilometers of historical radar data, we directly compare subsurface conditions with modern observations from NASA Operation IceBridge, NSF CReSIS, and ROSETTA-Ice.

Why does it matter? The Ross Ice Shelf buttresses glaciers that could raise global sea levels by 11.6 meters if lost. Understanding its long-term behavior is crucial for climate projections and coastal planning worldwide.

Why the Ross Ice Shelf?

Global Significance

Buttresses enough ice to raise global sea levels by over 11 meters if lost.

Extensive Data Coverage

Historic (1971–1979) and modern (2011–2017) radar surveys provide an unparalleled temporal baseline.

Map of Ross Ice Shelf showing survey lines and tectonic features.

Complex Dynamics

Long-term thinning and variable melting patterns reveal vulnerability to change.

Geological Control

Tectonic features shape diverse melt regimes beneath the ice shelf.

How We Study the Ross Ice Shelf from the Air

Step 1: Recovering Historical Data

1970s radar surveys recorded on film are scanned and digitized for analysis.

Step 2: Modern Surveys

Advanced digital radar systems collect detailed data about the ice today.

Step 3: Processing and Comparing Data

Old and new radar images are aligned, corrected, and compared to measure changes.

Step 4: Calculating Melt Rates

Mathematical models estimate melting and freezing at the ice shelf base.

Technical Notes

We use advanced algorithms to calibrate and compare old (SPRI/NSF/TUD) and modern (OIB/CReSIS, ROSETTA-Ice) radar data. Our methods include automated image interpretation and rigorous positioning corrections to ensure accurate measurements of ice thickness and basal melt rates.

Educational Impact & Broader Outreach

Student Engagement

High school through graduate students participate in authentic polar research, gaining hands-on experience with data analysis, algorithm development, and scientific publication processes.

Machine Learning Training

Student interpretation of radar film creates the largest training dataset for automated analysis algorithms, advancing both glaciology and computer vision applications.

Open Science

All calibrated datasets, analysis tools, and educational resources are publicly released, enabling global research community access to this unique historical archive.

About the Project

Frozen Legacies is a collaborative research initiative funded by the National Science Foundation (NSF) to investigate long-term changes beneath Antarctica’s Ross Ice Shelf using both historical and modern radar data.
